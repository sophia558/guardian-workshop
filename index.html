About This Session

Why Responsible AI Governance Matters

AI systems can make automated decisions that significantly impact people, communities and businesses — often at speed and scale. A single misconfigured system can cause widespread harm before anyone notices.

This workshop introduces Australia's Guidance for AI Adoption — a practical, six-principle framework designed to help any organisation deploy AI responsibly. You'll learn the framework, then apply it yourself to real-world scenarios.

The Framework

AI6 — Six Essential Practices

Each practice addresses a different dimension of responsible AI governance. Hover each card to highlight.

01

Decide Who Is Accountable

AI creates gaps where no one takes clear responsibility. Accountability must be explicitly assigned before anything is deployed.

  • Name a specific person responsible for every AI system
  • Define roles across the full supply chain — developers, deployers, third parties
  • Build AI literacy and training across the organisation
  • Document governance policies and consequences for non-compliance
Key Question
Who can be named right now?
02

Understand Impacts & Plan Accordingly

AI operates at speed and scale — potential harms are magnified. Plan for impacts before deployment, not after.

  • Identify all stakeholders who may be affected, including vulnerable groups
  • Assess potential harms, bias risks, and discriminatory outputs
  • Create clear, accessible channels for people to raise concerns
  • Monitor for new harms continuously throughout the lifecycle
Key Question
Who is harmed if this goes wrong?
03

Measure & Manage Risks

AI risks emerge from how systems behave in different situations — not just from software updates. Small issues can rapidly become systemic problems.

  • Build a fit-for-purpose AI risk management framework
  • Define your organisation's risk tolerance clearly
  • Conduct proportionate risk assessments for every AI system
  • Report and respond to incidents in a reasonable timeframe
Key Question
When was this last reviewed?
04

Share Essential Information

People should know when they're interacting with AI and understand when AI decisions affect them. Transparency builds trust.

  • Maintain an up-to-date AI register across the organisation
  • Communicate clearly when and how AI is being used
  • Explain the capabilities, limitations, and risks of each system
  • Be transparent about AI-generated content
Key Question
Can affected people understand this?
05

Test & Monitor

AI systems can change their behaviour over time. A system that worked well last month might behave differently today without anyone noticing.

  • Define clear acceptance criteria before deployment
  • Conduct thorough pre-deployment testing and document outcomes
  • Establish ongoing performance monitoring with defined metrics
  • Conduct regular audits proportionate to the level of risk
Key Question
Who sees the monitoring results?
06

Maintain Human Control

Unlike traditional software, AI learns patterns and makes opaque decisions. Humans must be able to oversee, intervene, and stop AI systems when needed.

  • Retain human oversight throughout the entire AI lifecycle
  • Ensure there is always a way to override or shut down the system
  • Train staff on when and how to intervene
  • Have a clear decommissioning plan when systems are retired
Key Question
Is override actually possible here?

Group Activity

How the Activity Works

Step 1

Pick Your Scenario

Your group will be assigned one of six AI scenarios. Read it carefully — think about who is involved, what the AI does, and what could go wrong.

Step 2

Apply the AI6

Work through each of the six practices as a group using the prompts in your scenario scorecard below. Don't skip any — even the tricky ones.

Step 3

Find the Hard Calls

Every scenario has edge cases. Discuss: what's the catastrophic risk? What's your group's risk tolerance? Where do you draw the line?

Step 4

Present Back

Each group shares their key findings with the room. What surprised you? Where did the framework challenge your assumptions?

AI Resume Ranking for Hiring
An AI system automatically processes resumes for a high-volume job role — scoring and ranking candidates based on keyword relevance, experience, and qualifications. A human recruiter reviews the ranked dashboard. The system has been running for 6 months without a formal review.
1
Accountability
HR leadership and the platform vendor are typically named. The key gap: does your organisation have a named individual accountable for the AI's scoring logic — not just the vendor contract? As the system evolves, who is checking it still performs fairly?
2
Understand Impacts
Key stakeholders: job applicants (especially from underrepresented groups), hiring managers, HR team, and the organisation's reputation. Core risk: bias baked into training data can systematically exclude qualified candidates before any human ever sees their application.
3
Measure & Manage Risks
Biggest risk: discriminatory screening at scale. Catastrophic scenario: the system has silently discriminated by gender or ethnicity for 6 months — legal exposure and reputational damage follow. Risk tolerance: low — someone's livelihood is at stake.
4
Share Essential Information
What to tell applicants: "Your application is initially reviewed by an AI ranking system before being assessed by a human recruiter." The AI register must document scoring criteria, data sources, known limitations, and bias test results.
5. Test & MonitorIs there a regular schedule to check the AI is still performing accurately and hasn't "gone off track"?

Group Debrief

Presenting Back to the Room

When you share back, don't just read your answers — share what surprised you, what your group disagreed on, and where the framework felt most challenging to apply.

  • Who did you name as accountable? Was it obvious, or did your group disagree?
  • What stakeholder did you almost miss? The most affected are often not the most visible.
  • What was your catastrophic risk? Did your group agree on the risk tolerance?
  • Yes or No on monitoring and human control? How many organisations would honestly answer Yes?
  • What would you change about this AI system to make it more responsibly governed?
